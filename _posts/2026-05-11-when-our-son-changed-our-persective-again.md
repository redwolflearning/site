With our daughter thriving in full-time virtual school, we expected to eventually follow a similar structure with our son. What we quickly realized, however, was that he was very different from his sister.

Where she tends to be quiet and easygoing, our son is strong-willed, energetic, and much more challenging in traditional settings. Before kindergarten, he was evaluated by the county and qualified for both an IEP and state-funded Pre-K due to social and behavioral delays. Even then, we could already see how intelligent he was.

Pre-K went incredibly well for him. His teacher understood him, connected with him, and helped him succeed. He had a wonderful year, and we truly believed he was ready for kindergarten.

Or so we thought.

By the end of the first week of school, he was already saying he did not want to go back. By Monday morning, he refused to get out of the car. Things escalated quickly after that. The classroom environment overwhelmed him, and he struggled to connect with his teacher. School became something he wanted to escape from, and his behaviors reflected that.

We had meetings with the school, sought support from professionals, and eventually received diagnoses of generalized anxiety and ADHD. The school made changes and tried different supports, including placing him in a resource room, but the behaviors continued.

Looking back now, we realize he was not acting out to hurt others or be malicious. He was overwhelmed, dysregulated, and trying to escape situations that felt too difficult for him to manage. What broke my heart most was the way he talked about himself afterward. He would say things like he was the worst, that everyone hated him, or that we should never have had him. It was devastating to watch a young child carry that kind of shame.

Eventually, we made the difficult decision to homeschool.

At the time, homeschooling felt impossible. I was still working full time while also trying to teach him how to read and build foundational math skills. This was completely outside my comfort zone. I had previously taught high school math, with Calculus and Geometry being some of my favorite subjects, and now work in high school math curriculum writing. Meanwhile, I was trying to figure out how to support both the emotional and academic needs of a young child who experienced learning so differently.

I felt completely lost.

But slowly, things began to change.

The resistance lessened. Not completely — he still argues about tasks sometimes — but he now works through them. Over time, we also realized that part of the problem in kindergarten was that he already understood much of the academic content being taught. He was frustrated, bored, and lacked the emotional regulation skills to communicate that appropriately.

Today, he is working above grade level in both math and language arts, though learning is still not without its challenges.

This experience reshaped my understanding of education once again. The school did not fail him, and I want to make that clear. Teachers and schools are carrying enormous responsibilities, and not every environment is able to meet every child exactly where they are academically, emotionally, and behaviorally.

What our son needed was flexibility, support, patience, and an environment that could respond to both his strengths and his struggles. Finding that path changed not only his learning journey, but our family’s perspective on education as a whole.

Today, our home includes one child attending public school virtually and one being homeschooled full time. While their educational paths look very different, both experiences have taught us the same thing: children thrive when they are supported, understood, and given opportunities to learn in ways that meet their individual needs.
