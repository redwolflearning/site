Both of my kids went through the phase of asking me endless math questions. I am not sure if this is just my house because they know I love math, but they would fire questions at me constantly: "What's 3 plus 7? What's 10 minus 2?" I won't lie—it got old, quick. Our adult brains are constantly juggling a thousand things we should be doing or need to do. Just when I wanted to say, "Okay, that's enough math for today," I flipped the script instead.

I told them I would answer their question if they answered mine first. And it worked! We would spend entire car rides going back and forth with math questions. Sometimes they would think they were going to trick me and ask something ridiculous like, "What's 100 plus 901?" To them, those numbers are huge. There is no way someone could do that in their head! When I came back with the answer, they were always impressed. (Mom points earned!) And if they do throw you a truly challenging one, you can always use a calculator without them seeing. 😉

Math was more challenging for our daughter, but she still loved this game. She plays it now with her younger brother, which has opened my eyes to how his brain works. He has a really keen number sense. On his own, he started adding three numbers together—a skill that isn't introduced until late 1st grade and isn't expected to be mastered until 2nd. He has even started adding numbers that are three digits! We haven't covered that yet in any formal way. I attribute a lot of this ability to using number bonds and focusing on meeting him where he was.

## Math is Everywhere

The "number question game" is just one way we interact with math in our daily lives. Another favorite? Sharing treats or toys. When we split up a bag of goldfish crackers or divide up LEGO pieces, we are naturally creating part-part-whole relationships—the foundation of number bonds.

We have also played counting games with toy cars where we make groups of 2, 3, 5, or 10, and then skip count with them to reach a goal like 100. There are also adorable [counting bears](https://amzn.to/4dLIvji) or [number cubes](https://amzn.to/4o2q6SF) you can use to make these activities feel even more hands-on.

Little activities like this feel like play to our kids, but they are quietly building the deep number sense that makes formal math so much easier later.

## Bringing Number Bonds Into Everyday Life

Once your child understands the "parts and whole" idea through snacks or toys, you can start to formalize it:

* Use hula hoops or [hopscotch rings](https://amzn.to/4dIBBeu) on the floor to make a giant number bond.
* Use string or yarn to make tabletop number bonds of all sizes.
* Print our free [large number bond mat](/site/resources/) and slide it into a [dry-erase pocket](https://amzn.to/4a2ILIc) to use over and over again.

The goal is not to drill facts—it is to help your child see the relationships between numbers so deeply that math just makes sense.
